The Awkward Middle Between Spreadsheets and ERP

March 11, 2026 — True Good Craft Inc.

Two things get recommended to small manufacturing shops when spreadsheets stop working. Use a better spreadsheet. Or go get an ERP. The first suggestion usually just gives you a nicer version of the same problem. The second costs more time, money, and effort than most small operations can justify, and that is before you even get into the ongoing pain of running it.

Most small shops know exactly how this goes. They outgrow the spreadsheet, look at ERP, realize what that actually means, and go right back to patching the spreadsheet. Not because it works. Because the alternatives are badly matched to the job.

That is the awkward middle. A lot of real operations live there.

Why spreadsheets stop scaling

The first problem is authority. A spreadsheet is not authoritative. Any cell can be changed by anybody, at any time, and sooner or later somebody overwrites something they should not. After enough of that, the numbers stop meaning much.

Then the patching starts. Formulas get bolted on. Columns get added. Sheets multiply. Before long you have a tab called "inventory v4 FINAL USE THIS ONE" and at least two older tabs that somebody is probably still using anyway. The workarounds pile up faster than the system gets cleaner.

Inventory drift comes next. You write down what came off the shelf, except for the time things got busy and you figured you would fix it later. You did not. Do that enough times and the count in the sheet stops matching the count on the shelf. Now you are buying, scheduling, and planning off numbers you do not trust.

Costing goes bad the same way. You estimated your material costs when you built the sheet. Prices changed. Your process changed. Waste crept in. The margin you thought you had got thinner, and nothing in the spreadsheet is going to tap you on the shoulder and tell you.

Then history starts disappearing. What did a specific run consume? What did it produce? What went wrong? What got substituted? If that information lived in a cell that got reused or overwritten the next time the file changed, it is gone.

Why ERP overshoots the problem

ERP is built for companies that can afford overhead. Setup overhead, admin overhead, money overhead, people overhead. That is fine for a bigger company. It is a bad fit for a small shop trying to keep stock, production, and costing straight.

The cost alone knocks it out for a lot of small operators. Licensing, setup, consulting, training, user fees, implementation partners. In some cases you can burn a shocking amount of time and money before the system is even useful.

And the work does not stop once it goes live. Permissions, updates, integrations, account management, general system babysitting. A small workshop usually does not have a dedicated person for that. More importantly, it should not need one.

The mismatch is not just cost. Most ERP systems are built for large organizations, layered workflows, and complexity that small shops simply do not have. If all you really need is clean inventory, repeatable production tracking, and usable costing, most ERP systems are solving a much bigger problem than the one in front of you.

What the awkward middle actually looks like

This is the reality for a lot of small manufacturing work. A workshop with a real product line. A maker or fabricator doing repeatable batches. Real materials. Real consumption. Real replenishment. Real production runs. Real costing.

This is not a hobby. There is real money on the line.

But it is also not a 200-person company. You do not need a giant procurement module, some elaborate multi-site workflow, or an enterprise portal for the sake of having one. You need to know what is on hand, what a run should use, what it actually used, what got built, and what it cost.

That is not a huge problem, but it is a real one. And the software options for it are usually either too flimsy or way too heavy.

Where BUS Core fits

BUS Core is built for that gap. It is local-first operational software for small manufacturing work. It runs on your own machine. Data stays local in SQLite. No cloud dependency, no subscription, no account required.

BUS Core is narrow on purpose.

You track inventory: what materials you have, what they cost, and what is on hand. You define Blueprints: what a run consumes and produces. You record Manufacturing Runs: what actually got built and what stock was really used. The Production Ledger keeps the history clean.

That is the loop.

It is not trying to do payroll, accounting, supplier portals, or every other enterprise function under the sun. It handles the operational layer that spreadsheets are bad at keeping straight and that ERP usually overcomplicates.

If you are building real things from real materials on a repeatable basis, and you want numbers you can actually trust, that is where BUS Core fits.

This is the niche

The awkward middle is full of real shops doing real work. Most stay on spreadsheets longer than they should because the alternative looks like ERP. Some go try ERP and find out, expensively, that it was the wrong fit.

BUS Core exists because that gap is real.

Not as a workaround. Not as a stripped-down enterprise suite. As a tool built for the actual scale of small manufacturing work.

When Spreadsheets Stop Working: The Messy Middle of Small Manufacturing

March 11, 2026 — True Good Craft Inc.

Most small shops start on spreadsheets because, frankly, that is the obvious move. They are fast, flexible, familiar, and free. If you are just trying to get product out the door and keep your head above water, a spreadsheet is good enough.

Until it isn't.

Usually the failure is not dramatic. You do not wake up one morning to a giant red warning light. What happens is slower and more annoying. You add a few more SKUs. A few more materials. A few more production runs. Then the spreadsheet stops being a useful tool and starts becoming a pile of assumptions.

What breaks first

Inventory is usually the first thing to drift.

The sheet says you have 4 metres of webbing. The shelf says you have 1.5. Somewhere along the line, something did not get recorded, got recorded late, or got adjusted by hand because it was easier in the moment. Now you are making purchasing and production decisions from numbers you do not trust.

That is the real problem. Not that the count is off. It is that the system has stopped being authoritative.

Next comes costing.

Early on, you can get away with rough numbers. Material cost plus a margin, maybe some guessed labour, call it close enough and move on. But as soon as the shop gets even slightly busier, that starts to rot. Material prices move. Recipes change. Waste shows up. Small process differences add up. You end up selling things at a price that feels reasonable, but you cannot say with a straight face what they actually cost to make.

Then history starts disappearing.

What did the last batch really consume? What did it actually produce? Did that run go sideways because of material loss, a substitution, or some odd one-off decision on the bench? If the answer lives in somebody's head, or in a spreadsheet cell that got reused three weeks later, then for practical purposes it is gone.

What you're seeing is called inventory drift

If your numbers don't match reality, you're already dealing with inventory drift.

Read: Inventory Drift explained

Why ERP is not the answer

This is usually where somebody says, "Sounds like you need an ERP."

They are not completely wrong. They have identified the category of problem. They have just overshot the scale of the solution by a country mile.

Most small shops do not need an enterprise system. They do not need implementation consultants, admin overhead, cloud accounts, training plans, and a software bill that acts like a second utility payment. They need a system that can reliably track what comes in, what gets consumed, what gets built, and what it cost. That is it.

The usual ERP answer is technically valid and operationally ridiculous.

The gap is real

There is a real gap between "good luck with your spreadsheet" and "welcome to enterprise software hell." A lot of small manufacturing shops live right there. They are building real products, in real batches, with real material costs, but they do not have the size or appetite for heavyweight software.

That is the gap BUS Core is built for.

BUS Core is local-first operational software for small manufacturing work. It runs on your own machine. Data stays local in SQLite. There is no cloud dependency, no monthly fee, and no account circus. You install it, open a browser tab to localhost:8765, and start working.

The operating loop is simple on purpose:

  • materials go in as inventory
  • Blueprints define what a run consumes and produces
  • Manufacturing Runs record what was actually built
  • stock gets deducted from real usage
  • costs come from actual inputs instead of vibes
  • the Production Ledger keeps the history

None of that is glamorous. Good. It should not be. This kind of software should be boring in the right ways. The value is not novelty. The value is that the numbers stay tied to reality run after run.

A practical threshold

If you are regularly finding stock mismatches, second-guessing your pricing, or realizing you cannot cleanly explain what your last production run cost, that is the point where the spreadsheet has stopped scaling with the operation.

You do not need a giant system. You need a smaller one that is actually built for the job.

BUS Core is open source under the AGPL. The current release is on the downloads page. If you want to follow along, ask questions, or poke holes in it, the Reddit community and Discord are there.

The messy middle is real. Spreadsheet pain is real. The answer does not have to be enterprise software.

When Spreadsheets Stop Working: The Messy Middle of Small Manufacturing

March 11, 2026 — True Good Craft Inc.

Most small manufacturing shops start on spreadsheets. This is the right call. A spreadsheet is fast to set up, flexible, and free. For a small operation just finding its footing, it works.

Then the operation grows slightly. Not dramatically — just slightly. A few more SKUs. A couple of new materials. An extra production run per week. And the spreadsheet starts to show cracks.

What breaks first

It is rarely one dramatic failure. It is a slow accumulation of small problems.

Inventory drift is usually the first sign. The spreadsheet says you have 4 metres of webbing. You go to the shelf and find 1.5. Somewhere between the last three production runs, the tracking fell behind. You are not sure when. It does not matter when — what matters is that you are now making decisions on bad numbers.

Pricing comes next. Early on, you estimated your material cost and added a margin. That estimate made sense at the time. But material prices changed, your recipe changed slightly, and the labour component was always a guess. You are now selling product at a price that may or may not cover your actual cost. You do not know for certain. This is an uncomfortable position to be in as volume grows.

Then there is batch history. What did the last run of that product actually cost? Which materials were consumed? Was there anything unusual about that run? If the answers live in memory or in a cell you might have overwritten, they are effectively gone.

Why ERP is not the answer

At this point, a well-meaning person will suggest an ERP system. They are not wrong about the category of problem. They are wrong about the scale of solution.

ERP systems are designed for organizations with dedicated staff, implementation budgets, and operational complexity that justifies the overhead. A small shop making bags, enclosures, or fabricated parts does not have any of those things. An ERP system requires more maintenance than the operation it is meant to support. It is the wrong tool.

The gap has a name

The space between a spreadsheet and an ERP system is not empty. It is just underserved. It is the operational reality of a small shop that builds real products in real batches and needs structured tracking without enterprise overhead.

This is where BUS Core sits.

BUS Core is local-first operational software. It runs on your machine. Your data stays in a local SQLite database. There is no cloud sync, no subscription, no account required. You install it, you open a browser tab to localhost:8765, and you start entering your materials and recipes.

The core workflow is structured around the same loop every small manufacturing operation runs:

  • Materials go in as inventory.
  • Recipes (Blueprints) define what a production run consumes and produces.
  • A Manufacturing Run records what was actually built and deducts materials automatically.
  • Costs are calculated from real inputs, not estimates.
  • The Production Ledger holds the history.

That loop is not complicated. But enforcing it consistently — run after run, week after week — is where spreadsheets fail and where structured tooling pays off.

A practical starting point

If your operation has reached the point where inventory drift is a regular problem, or where you genuinely do not know the cost of your last production run, that is the signal. You do not need a large system. You need a small, reliable one.

The gap between spreadsheets and ERP is real. It has a practical solution.
